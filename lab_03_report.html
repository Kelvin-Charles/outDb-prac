Task 03: Advanced Database Operations

Objective:

To demonstrate advanced SQL operations including calculated fields, complex joins, and conditional filtering using the university database.

Instructions:

This task involves performing advanced database operations including salary calculations, multi-table joins, and conditional queries. All operations were performed using MariaDB 10.4.32 in a Docker container environment.

Step a) Calculate 20% salary raise for each instructor (without modifying data)

SQL Query:

SELECT name, salary, (salary * 1.20) AS new_salary
FROM instructor
ORDER BY salary DESC;
Figure 3a: Salary calculation showing current and new salaries with 20% raise

Result:

The query calculates a 20% salary increase for each instructor without modifying the actual database records. This demonstrates the use of calculated fields and arithmetic operations in SELECT statements. The results are ordered by current salary in descending order.

Step b) Retrieve instructor names with department and building information

SQL Query:

SELECT i.name, i.dept_name, d.building
FROM instructor i
JOIN department d ON i.dept_name = d.dept_name
ORDER BY i.name;
Figure 3b: All instructors with their department names and building locations

Result:

The query successfully joins the instructor and department tables to display comprehensive information about each instructor including their name, department, and building location. This demonstrates the use of INNER JOIN to combine related data from multiple tables.

Step c) Find all instructors who have taught courses with their names and course IDs

SQL Query:

SELECT DISTINCT i.name, t.course_id
FROM instructor i
JOIN teaches t ON i.ID = t.ID
ORDER BY i.name, t.course_id;
Figure 3c: All instructors who teach courses with their assigned course IDs

Result:

The query identifies all instructors who have teaching assignments and displays their names along with the course IDs they teach. This demonstrates the use of JOIN operations to establish relationships between instructors and their teaching responsibilities, with DISTINCT to eliminate duplicate entries.

Step d) Retrieve Computer Science instructors with salary less than 7,500,000 TZS

SQL Query:

SELECT name, salary, dept_name
FROM instructor
WHERE dept_name = 'Computer Science' 
  AND salary < 7500000
ORDER BY salary;
Figure 3d: Computer Science instructors earning less than 7,500,000 TZS

Result:

The query filters Computer Science department instructors based on salary criteria, showing only those earning less than 7,500,000 TZS. This demonstrates the use of multiple conditions in WHERE clause with AND operator for precise data filtering based on department and salary constraints.
